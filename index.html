Problem Statement

Fragmented schedules create cognitive overload.

University students manage classes, assignments, personal commitments, and transport across disconnected tools such as Canvas, Blackboard, calendars, WhatsApp, and HA Go. This fragmentation causes repeated checking, missed deadlines, and constant mental recalibration when schedules change.

Design challenge: How might we help students consolidate academic and life schedules while reducing mental load and improving planning confidence?

Research Findings

Pain vs. solution

Through the contextual inquiries and the artifacts, we understand more about the design pain points in the existing applications. We matched the pain points to our solution and synthesised three key tasks to address in our solution.

Fragmented sources Users repeatedly cross-check announcements and calendars across Canvas, chat, and calendars.
→
Design implication

Unify sources with automated synchronization so one view can be trusted.

Mental recalibration Participants missed updates when plans shifted quickly; ad-hoc changes broke their “mental model” of the week.
→
Design implication

Add predictive alerting and visible conflict warnings when schedules change.

Life + academic overload Users described stress when all events look equally urgent in one mixed feed.
→
Design implication

Separate academic and life contexts while preserving fast switching.

Hidden workload Participants could not estimate upcoming weekly pressure until it was too late.
→
Design implication

Provide workload overview and semantic urgency cues for at-a-glance triage.

Synthesis outcome

3 Key User Tasks

These three tasks translate research evidence into product requirements : calendar unification, workload intelligence, and conflict-aware planning.

Task 1

Calendar function for academic & personal use

  • Tab and color grouping to separate event types (e.g., assignments vs. life events).
  • External calendar import via third-party platform APIs or manual uploads.
  • Multiple views: day, week, month, to-do tasks, and customized layouts.

Task 2

Workload scheduler & work notification

  • Provide an assignment workload overview.
  • Sync and refresh the assignment calendar frequently for exact submission dates and times.
  • Score to-dos by importance and remaining time.
  • Push deadline alerts to phone notifications.

Task 3

Event planner

  • Surface alerts for upcoming academic events before adding a personal event.
  • Suggest time slots when adding new user event
  • Allow changing dates/times of selected events based on the user’s mood.

Design Synthesis

From Insights to Goals

By synthesizing feedback from our individual high-fidelity evaluations, we identified critical usability gaps. These insights were formalized into four core pillars that define and prioritise the architecture of our integrated solution.

1

Consolidate fragmented sources

Why this matters

Users flagged manual data entry as a severe barrier, feeling drained by repetitive input across Canvas and external calendars.

Design move

Prioritize automated platform synchronization through Canvas and Google Calendar integration as the core workflow to eliminate manual friction.

Success signal Users can answer “Am I free?” from one screen without app-switching fatigue.
2

Reduce cognitive load

Why this matters

Fragmented views and schedule changes triggered constant re-checking and hidden clashes that users could not see in time.

Design move

Embed automated conflict detection and real-time alerts into the planning flow to reduce mental recalibration.

Success signal Fewer overlapping commitments are identified during walkthrough tasks.
3

Mitigate academic anxiety

Why this matters

Mixed views caused high levels of anxiety and psychological burden when workloads and personal events were jumbled together.

Design move

Implement segregated context views using a toggleable Academic versus Life tab system to protect mental boundaries.

Success signal Users can focus on study or rest blocks without visual noise or overwhelm.
4

Clarify urgency and workload

Why this matters

Students struggled to gauge remaining work across courses, often discovering high-risk tasks too late.

Design move

Establish a strict semantic color system and workload indicators such as percentages to communicate urgency instantly.

Success signal High-risk tasks and semester progress are recognized at a glance without reading details.

Evaluation

What testing revealed

We conducted several usability testings with the target participants to validate UniFlow’s impact on planning efficiency and psychological load. The testing focused on task completion for automated syncing, conflict resolution, and navigation.

The table below summarizes the key findings from user testing, categorized by issue, evidence, and severity to guide our iteration priorities.

Issue Evidence Severity
Editing Flow Users found the event-list editing flow lengthy and overly multi-pathed. Major
Course Info Clarity Users were confused by course-page layout, deadline meaning, and assignment weight visibility. Major
Delete Controls Users could not delete events, event types, or course information. Major
Plan Suggestions Users wanted planning suggestions for normal events, not only conflict cases. Minor
Task Categories Users wanted more specific task categories beyond broad labels like Life and Academic. Minor

Feature gallery

The final prototype brings together the most impactful features from our high-fidelity designs, focusing on fragmentation, cognitive load, anxiety, and urgency. Below are the key features refined through synthesis and iteration.

Urgency semantics

A strict color system helps users read task urgency instantly, with warning states highlighting overdue, due-soon, or conflicted items.

Supports Task 2 · Clarify urgency without extra reading.

Weekly view

The list page is reorganised by week so users can scan upcoming deadlines faster and understand short-term workload at a glance.

Supports Task 1 · Improve readability and planning flow.

Dynamic Course Dashboards

Course cards summarise task weighting, deadline, status, and progress so students can monitor each module without opening every item one by one.

Supports Task 2 · Strengthen course-level awareness.

Dual-Sector Categorization

Separate Academic and Life views reduce visual clutter while keeping fast switching available through tabs and filters.

Supports Task 1 · Reduce cognitive load from mixed schedules.

Conflict detection

Overlapping events are flagged immediately so users can spot clashes early and adjust plans before they break the schedule.

Supports Task 3 · Prevent scheduling conflicts.

Smart sync

Sync controls bring updates from external platforms into one planner, reducing repetitive manual checking across course and personal sources while keeping update changes visible in one place.

Supports Task 1 · Consolidate fragmented sources.

Workload overview

Semester progress, course breakdowns, and upcoming task dues help students judge what needs attention first across the week and term.

Supports Task 2 · Make workload visible and actionable.

Rich add-event context

Expanded inputs such as category, notes, and course-related details help users record tasks with clearer context and less ambiguity later.

Supports Task 3 · Improve planning confidence.

Reminder controls

Adjustable reminder timing gives users more control over when and how often they are alerted before important deadlines.

Supports Task 2 · Reduce missed submissions.

Reflection and Next Steps

Reflection

What the team learned

Different users’ needs vary

Although our target users’ scope is already focused on university students, we found that their needs and priorities are quite different.

For example, some minimalists want a clean and only contains the basic functions, while some want one with more functionalities.

We have to make a trade-off, as we cannot make everyone happy. But the process is hard.


User behavior ≠ designer expectation

There are some features that we thought the user would like, but actually didn’t. While the user appreciates some features, we think they’re really easy (custom color pattern). So we know that designers sometimes can not feel what users feel.


Is the user always right?

This is the philosophy question that we think about throughout the entire process. We know that there have been many times in history when people have made tremendous products without listening to users’ ideas, such as the iPhone and iPad.

Although we are not as intelligent as Steve Jobs, should we always listen to the user?

Next steps

Future improvements

Core interaction features

Due to the limited project timeline, we focused on implementing the main interaction features of the application.

Features such as theme enhancement and animated mascots were deferred to future work so that we could prioritize the core interaction patterns and gather meaningful user feedback on the concept.


Sync & Accounts

As this project is currently a Figma prototype, it focuses on interaction design rather than full system implementation. Features such as Canvas event import and account synchronization are therefore left as future work for a functional version of the app.

Appendix

Interview protocol, consent, and demo links

In the user case study sessions, we followed the approved User Study Consent Form (preview at right). Interview questions were aligned with course ethics expectations; artifacts in this portfolio trace back to that documented participation.

Contextual Inquiry Interview questions

  1. What application do you allocate the time slot to in your daily life?
  2. Why do you use multiple applications/methods to manage your schedule?
  3. How often do you check your schedule tools in a week?
  4. Can you try to show me how you use Google Calendar?
  5. Do you find any issues with Google Calendar in your daily use?
  6. Can you try to show me how you use the calendar function in Blackboard?
  7. Do you find any issues with calendar functions in Blackboard in your daily use?
  8. If there is an app that combines the calendar function of daily life and academic use, what would you expect from this app?
  9. How is your life this semester? Could you walk me through what a typical week looks like for you?
  10. When you receive a new event or deadline (e.g., from Canvas, WhatsApp, or email), what do you usually do with that information?
  11. Can you also show me how you do it? (e.g., if you just got a new group meeting time on WhatsApp, what are the exact steps you take?)
  12. Why do you use multiple applications/methods to manage your schedule? Is it because of how information is sent to you, or because each tool is good at something different?
  13. When you want to know Am I free at this time?, what do you currently do, step by step?
  14. Has there been a time when you thought you were free, but later realised you actually had something scheduled? Can you tell me more about it?
  15. What are the most frustrating moments in your current time-management process?
  16. So you mentioned the ad-hoc cases and last-minute changes like sudden hospital appointments/rescheduled group meetings, how would you like the app to handle them?
  17. Is there anything about how you manage your time this semester that we have not talked about, but you think is important for us to understand to design a better system for you?
